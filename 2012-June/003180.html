Afaik, the CA compiles the cert and signs (internal hash) over it.
eg: CA's moving to sha1 for that because internal md5 is broken.
Afaik, the internal hash is different than the external 'fingerprint'.
Need to read the x509 stuff again to be sure.

Anyways, on to apps. The fetchmail 'fingerprint', be it md5 or sha1,
is over the DER encoded form of the cert, openssl x509 -outform der

Yes, other fine hash algos exist. But:
1) You can't expect to interop with the rest of the world if you're
using rmd160 or some sha3 finalist, etc as your only external
hash... because everyone else searches for, lists and speaks sha1.
And md5 for legacy situations.
2) SHA-3 will be final by year end. If the industry moves from
sha1 (whether broken or not) they'll move to SHA-3 (because
among strong hashes, business likes being able to pass the
buck to the gov't endorsed one.)

Since sha1 and md5 already need to be in apps, and if the user
wants more security than those are currently [un]known to provide,
I'd suggest that the fingerprint check 'AND' both their test results
together. Or do the same but with sha3 when it comes out.
